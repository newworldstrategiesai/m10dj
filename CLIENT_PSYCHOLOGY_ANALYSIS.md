# Why Clients Choose Package 2 But Want to Alter It

## The Client's Journey

**Client's SMS:**
1. "I like package 2"
2. "We won't need the monogram projection" (already removed)
3. "Can we remove the extra hour?"

**What this tells us:**

---

## Root Cause Analysis

### 1. **Price Anchoring, Not Value Matching**

**What happened:**
- Client sees Package 2: $2,500
- Thinks: "This is my budget" or "This seems reasonable"
- Then sees what's included
- Thinks: "I don't need all of this for $2,500"

**The psychology:**
- She anchored to the $2,500 price point FIRST
- Then evaluated if the contents matched that price
- Found items she didn't perceive as valuable
- Wanted to "trim the fat" to match her perceived value

**Why this happens:**
- Package 1 ($2,000) might feel too basic
- Package 3 ($3,000) might feel too expensive
- Package 2 ($2,500) = "sweet spot" price
- But the contents don't match her specific needs

---

### 2. **"Most Popular" Creates FOMO, Not Necessity**

**What happened:**
- "Most Popular" tag made her think: "This must be what I need"
- But she didn't actually evaluate if she needed everything
- She trusted the recommendation, then questioned the details

**The psychology:**
- Social proof ("Most Popular") = "Others chose this, so I should too"
- But then individual evaluation = "Wait, do I actually need this?"

**Why this happens:**
- "Most Popular" implies it's the right choice
- But it doesn't mean it's the right choice FOR HER
- She's following the crowd, not her actual needs

---

### 3. **Lack of Understanding: "Why Do I Need This?"**

**Specific items she questioned:**

**A. "Extra Hour"**
- Package says "Up to 6 hours"
- Breakdown shows: "4 Hours" + "Additional Hour" = 5 hours
- She thinks: "Why do I need an extra hour? My event is only 4 hours"
- Reality: The "extra hour" might be for ceremony/cocktail hour separation
- But she doesn't understand WHY it's included

**B. "Extra Speaker"**
- She might think: "I only have one room, why do I need two speakers?"
- Reality: It's for ceremony/cocktail hour separation
- But if her ceremony and cocktail hour are in the same space, she doesn't see the value

**The psychology:**
- She sees items, not solutions
- She doesn't understand the "why" behind each item
- Items feel like "add-ons" not "necessities"

---

### 4. **Package 1 Doesn't Have Enough, Package 3 Has Too Much**

**The gap:**
- Package 1: "Reception Only" - might not include ceremony
- Package 2: "Complete Wedding" - includes everything
- Package 3: "Premium Experience" - includes special effects

**What she needs:**
- Probably needs ceremony + reception
- Doesn't need all the extras in Package 2
- But Package 1 might not cover ceremony

**The psychology:**
- She's stuck between packages
- Package 2 is the "next step up" from what she needs
- But it includes things she doesn't want
- She's trying to create "Package 1.5"

---

### 5. **Transparency Creates Optimization, Not Trust**

**What happened:**
- She can see the breakdown
- She can do the math: $2,500 for these items
- She thinks: "If I remove $300, I should pay $2,200"
- She's optimizing for price, not value

**The psychology:**
- Transparency = "I can see what I'm paying for"
- But it also = "I can see what I DON'T need"
- She's treating it like a shopping cart, not a package

**Why this happens:**
- Humans optimize when given options
- She sees items as "optional" not "included"
- The breakdown makes it feel customizable

---

### 6. **Budget Constraint, Not Value Alignment**

**What happened:**
- She might have a budget around $2,200-$2,500
- Package 2 is $2,500 (at the top of her budget)
- She wants to get it lower to have buffer
- Or she wants to stay under a specific number

**The psychology:**
- Budget is fixed: "I can spend $2,500 max"
- Package is $2,500: "Perfect, but can I get it cheaper?"
- Removing items = "Getting a better deal"

**Why this happens:**
- Budgets are often rigid
- She's trying to maximize value within her budget
- Removing items feels like "saving money" not "losing value"

---

### 7. **Comparison Shopping Behavior**

**What happened:**
- She might be comparing to other DJs
- Other DJs might offer "custom packages" or "build your own"
- She thinks: "Why can't I just get what I need?"

**The psychology:**
- Market expectations: "I should be able to customize"
- Package feels restrictive
- She wants flexibility, not structure

**Why this happens:**
- Modern consumers expect customization
- Packages feel "one-size-fits-all"
- She wants "one-size-fits-me"

---

## The Real Problem

**She's not buying a package. She's buying a price point.**

1. She anchored to $2,500
2. She evaluated if the contents matched that price
3. She found items she didn't value
4. She wanted to remove them to "optimize" the price

**She's treating it like:**
- A shopping cart (remove items, price goes down)
- A negotiation (start high, remove items, get lower price)
- A custom build (pick what I need, pay for what I get)

**Not like:**
- A curated experience (this is what you need for a complete wedding)
- A value bundle (you save money by taking everything)
- A solution (this solves your problem)

---

## What This Means for Your Pricing

### The Good News:
- She WANTS Package 2 (the price point is right)
- She TRUSTS your recommendation ("Most Popular")
- She's ENGAGED (asking questions, not walking away)

### The Bad News:
- She doesn't see VALUE in all the items
- She's OPTIMIZING for price, not outcomes
- She's TREATING it like a shopping cart

### The Opportunity:
- Reframe the conversation
- Explain the "why" behind each item
- Show value, not just features
- Make it feel like a solution, not a list

---

## Solutions Based on This Analysis

### Solution 1: **Explain the "Why"**

**Instead of:**
- "Additional Hour DJ/MC Services - $300"

**Say:**
- "Additional Hour: Ensures seamless transitions between ceremony, cocktail hour, and reception. Most couples find they need this buffer time for photos, guest arrival, or unexpected delays. Without it, you risk rushing between events or paying overtime fees."

**Result:** She understands WHY it's included, not just WHAT it is.

---

### Solution 2: **Address the Gap Between Packages**

**Create messaging:**
- "Package 1 is perfect if you're having a separate ceremony or intimate reception."
- "Package 2 is designed for couples who want everything in one seamless flow."
- "If you're unsure, Package 2 is the safest choice - most couples end up adding these services anyway."

**Result:** She understands why Package 2 exists and why it's "complete."

---

### Solution 3: **Reframe Removals as "Risks"**

**Instead of:**
- "Sure, we can remove the extra hour."

**Say:**
- "I understand you're thinking about the extra hour. Here's why it's included: [explanation]. If we remove it, you risk [specific risk]. Most couples find it's worth the peace of mind. But if your timeline is very tight and you're confident, we can discuss alternatives."

**Result:** Removals feel like trade-offs, not savings.

---

### Solution 4: **Show Value, Not Just Price**

**Instead of:**
- "Package 2: $2,500 (Save $900)"

**Say:**
- "Package 2: Complete Wedding Experience - $2,500"
- "This package is designed for couples who want to relax and enjoy their day, knowing everything is handled. The $2,500 includes full-day coverage, professional sound separation, and complete lighting - everything you need for a seamless wedding."

**Result:** She sees value in the experience, not just savings on items.

---

## Key Insight

**She chose Package 2 because:**
- The price felt right ($2,500)
- The tag said "Most Popular" (social proof)
- It seemed like the "next step up" from Package 1

**She wanted to alter it because:**
- She didn't understand WHY certain items were included
- She saw items as "optional" not "necessary"
- She was optimizing for price, not value
- She was treating it like a shopping cart, not a solution

**The fix:**
- Lead with experience, not items
- Explain the "why" behind inclusions
- Frame removals as risks, not savings
- Show value in outcomes, not features

---

## Action Items

1. **Add "Why" explanations** to each package item
2. **Create messaging** that addresses the gap between packages
3. **Reframe removals** as trade-offs, not savings
4. **Show value** in outcomes, not just price
5. **Train team** on value-focused responses

The goal: Make her WANT the complete package because she understands the value, not just because it's the "most popular" price point.
